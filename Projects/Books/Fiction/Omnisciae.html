Prologue

The Keeper of All Stories

As white as pristine paper yet to be inked.

As bright as the full moon in a clear sky.

As delicate as a snowshoe hare.

As sophisticated as fine marble.

As pure as a white rose.

As organic as ivory.

As warm as love.

As loyal as faith.

As true as light.

As black as a shadow.

As dark as the night.

As untamed as ivy.

As raw as chaff.

As maligned as a crow.

As artificial as glass.

As vile as lust.

As murky as the Styx.

As deceiving as Dolos.

As empyrean as heaven.

As brave as the stars.

As unforgiving as the nether.

As capricious as Mars.

The past and the future.

The will be-s and the will not be-s.

Every opposite and in-between was gathered here.

In this one place.

And I was here. At their junction.

The twilight sky at the horizon merged in an unnatural gradient with the void-black below and the blue sky above.

All that could be stood at the horizon, out of reach, and all that was was me.

In the middle of it all.

Alone.

I thought this was reality, unchanging solitude. A mockery of freedom. My heart held no affection, no feelings for this place. I felt as empty as the space before me.

But then, there was that first meeting. An amorphous collection of colours that shifted, hinting at a humanoid form - it morphed out of thin air, as if the twilight were just a painting meters away. When it spoke, the tired voice sounded mere inches from my ears.

“Painful, isn’t it?” The words were devoid of any emotion - save perhaps a degree of fatigue with a dash of apathy. “The void is a sad place. Unchanging, unfeeling.” For the first time I felt something: pain. A desire to be free.

There was so much more, out of my reach. The creature drew near. It was within arm’s reach when it resumed, a tone of satisfaction. “Your wish has been heard.”

It announced, and with its words the scenery shifted abruptly. The horizon rose upwards, a wall of books in its place enclosing the space. Loud clangs echoed as the empty sky-void was replaced with towering bookshelves, and a spiraling staircase snaked upwards in the center of it all.

“Welcome to Omnisciae.”

Abruptly, I was freed from my stasis, bracing my arms just in time to soften the impact as I hit the cold stone floor. Nausea and the utter incomprehension of my predicament bid me sit down, but the figure, unheeding my toil, had already begun its ascent. I lumbered after it with unsteady steps, climbing onto the first step. Silence reigned once more as we climbed up, I much too weak to speak. Ominous sounds echoed down from the heights of the tower I could not see, and it did not take long for the ground, too, to be lost from view. The staircase went on and on, step after step.

Despite its size, the creature moved lightly, swiftly where the wood groaned under my feet. Every so often, it would pause briefly, inspecting the spine of a book on the distant wall, before resuming its ascent just as suddenly. I’m not sure how long passed before I felt well enough to speak, but at last I gave word to my confusion.

“Where are we?”

The creature froze, and without turning to face me, responded.

“Omnisciae”

“And where’s that?”

I intended to sound indignant for the non-answer but I imagine it sounded more scared or desperate. The creature didn’t answer, but instead raised its pseudo-arm into the air. A tremor swept through the tower, each book quivered in its slot. Somewhere in the far distance, there was a response.

The walls or the library bent inwards, distorting the space until the book was within the figure’s reach. Casually, it retrieved the volume, and all in a moment the library had returned to as it was. Finally, the creature turned to face me.

“Everything that has ever existed, is recorded here. Everything that will ever exist is recorded here. Omnisciae is a record of reality.”

The answer was little more help than its predecessor, but atleast the creature seemed willing to engage in dialogue. I ventured another question, “How do I go back?”

“To the void?”

“No… Somewhere else.”

“Depends… Where to?”

“I…” I wasn’t sure. But I did not belong here. Something felt… alien.

“I’m not supposed to be here.” My voice was little more than a whisper, but the creature heard it.

“That you are not.”

It’s response had a greater satisfaction, bordering on excitement as it approached me. I stared expectantly, but it did not elaborate, and instead quietly handed me the book it had obtained. A leather cover bound the thick pages, dyed in royal purple, the title embossed in fine cursive gold lettering.

‘Alaric of Cerebith’ it read.

The names were not familiar, neither the person’s, nor what I presumed was the place they came from.

“Every book here chronicles the life of a person in the world.” The creature spoke as I opened the book, leafing through the pages. The story was unremarkable, though recorded with excruciating detail. Every thought, every action.

And then it happened. I turned a page only to reveal its successors blank. I turned one, then another, then another, and another. I skimmed through all the pages till I hit the back cover. They were all empty.

I looked back up at the creature in confusion. There was no explanation, but I had an instinctive understanding of what this meant, what changing a record of reality would mean. I recalled the creature’s first words, that I was the first visitor that Omnisciae had had. The series of deductions lead me to only one possible conclusion:

“You erased Alaric’s story.”

It wasn’t a question. It was an outright accusation.

“You killed them.”

The whisper of a laugh emanated from the creature, as it took a step back.

The staircase shook, and the walls around me began descending - no, the staircase was perhaps snaking upwards once again. When it stopped, around me were broken bookshelves, battered books, pages strewn about. More than a book was missing from its slot.

“Now, now. You have good instincts, but don’t trust them so. I haven’t killed anyone. As a matter of fact, I have erased them entirely from the world.”

“That’s even worse!” Not just to snuff a life out, but to make it so they never existed. Could there be a greater death?

“A person who never existed cannot be killed.”

“But they did exist, the proof is right here!”

The desperation in my voice mounted as I flung open the book. There were still pages with text on them. The story was not entirely lost.

“It appears I have missed a few pages. That… is a problem. There cannot be an incomplete story.” The creature mused, not at all alarmed by the idea that it was the perpetrator of a crime so irreversible, so hideously unfair. Indeed, it seemed far more perturbed by the notion that Omnisciae might be incomplete.

“That’s not-” I began, but it interrupted me, a most unearthly smile plastered on its inhuman face. “Which is why you are here.”

It was my turn to pause. Accepting the silence, it continued,

“Every person in the world that exists in recorded here. Yet, there is no book for you. You do not belong here. You simply do not exist.” There was a mock sorrow in its voice as it described my predicament. “A being with no book, no story. Oh how regretful, that you shall never have existed.”

It took a step closer, its fingers lifting my chin to face it, and it whispered, “But there is a solution. There is an empty book.”

Finally, I realised what it meant, what was going on. I recoiled from it, horrified.

“You created me to take his place?” My voice was already a shout, and the silence amplified it. Still, the creature stood perfectly assured in its plans.

“Alaric was an anomaly. His story did not match with this world. This is simply a correction. Live a life that can fill these pages. That is all I need from you.”

“You want me accomplice to his murder!”

“He never existed!” The voice was closer, louder, annoyed. “Don’t make me repeat myself, Alaric.”

“Don’t call me that name.”

“Then pray tell what is your name?” It boomed, mocking my dilemma, “You would rather remain as you are, a transient anomaly with no name, no body, till you return to the nothingness from whence you came? You would return to that featureless void that I saved you from? All for what, a person who never existed to begin with?”

I couldn’t find my voice, and its honeyed assurances filled the vacuum of my retort.

“You have no choice. No one would blame you. What do you owe anyways? Just accept. There is no crime, you are he.”

“And your puppet.” I rebutted, finding my voice. I could not deny the temptation, the innate urge to accept what was my only chance at existing, escaping that void, accept the book, accept wanting to be real. I needed something else, something else to dissuade myself.

“Ofcourse not.” It feigned pain, as if I had betrayed it by alleging so. “You would be free to live your life however you please. That is why I erased so many stories: the stories that came after Alaric. Now, you can live as you please, and all the stories shall be rewritten.” It motioned upwards, an infinite number of books above us in a similar state.

“Accept. There is no recourse. Only you can save it all. It is the right thing to do.”

 I found myself trembling, with not only apprehension but a trickle of excitement. I disgusted me. But, it was right. This was the right thing to do. I had no choice. I was the victim here.

“But how do I know you won’t erase me, too?” I asked the final question, and the creature pointed to the book.

“Take it with you.”

Understanding, I nodded slowly. With the book in my possession, it could not control me.

“Goodbye, Alaric.” The voice boomed, and the scenery shifted once more, I hugged the book to my chest, stifling the protests in my heart. This was for the best. I would make it count.

The library and the creature receded from view till the world shifted to black.
